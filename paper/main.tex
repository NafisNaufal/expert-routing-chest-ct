\title{Grounded Vision--Language Adaptation on Chest CT and Reports for
Clinically-Oriented Zero-Shot Abnormality Detection and Case Retrieval}

\author{Dionisius Seraf Saputra$^{1}$, Nafis Naufal Rahman$^{2}$}

\affil{$^1$Department of Information System, Universitas Brawijaya, Malang, Indonesia}
\affil{$^2$Department of Informatics Engineering, Universitas Brawijaya, Malang, Indonesia}

\email{dionisiusve32@student.ub.ac.id, nafisnaufal1426@student.ub.ac.id}

\begin{abstract}
Manual annotation of 3D medical modalities such as Chest Computed Tomography
(CT) is highly labour-intensive, limiting the scalability of deep learning
systems for thoracic diagnosis. While Vision-Language Models (VLMs) show
significant promise in 2D imaging, applying them natively to volumetric CT
data remains challenging due to memory constraints and the difficulty of
fine-grained spatial localisation. This paper presents a parameter-efficient
adaptation framework built on VILA-M3, an expert-routed medical VLM developed
under the MONAI project. The framework is adapted to thoracic clinical tasks
via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on the large-scale CT-RATE dataset, aligning
the model's language representations with chest CT visual patterns and radiology
report semantics. A built-in dynamic routing mechanism delegates high-resolution
volumetric localisation to VISTA3D, a specialist 3D segmentation expert, while
the VLM retains semantic control. Zero-shot transferability of the adapted model
is evaluated on the LIDC-IDRI reference database for pulmonary nodule
localisation and on a CT-RATE held-out split for natural language-based case
retrieval. This work demonstrates the practical viability of expert-routed VLM
adaptation as a scalable approach to clinical decision support that substantially
reduces reliance on densely annotated training data.
\end{abstract}


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Chest CT is a primary imaging modality for diagnosing thoracic diseases
including pulmonary nodules, pneumonia, pleural effusion, and cardiovascular
conditions. Despite advances in deep learning, high-performing models in this
domain still depend on large datasets annotated with dense bounding boxes or
pixel-level segmentation masks---an expensive process requiring considerable
radiological expertise and time~\cite{litjens2017}.

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) offer an alternative by learning visual
representations from the natural pairing of medical images and clinical
free-text reports, bypassing the need for explicit spatial labels~\cite{clip2021,
medclip2022}. Early medical VLMs such as MedCLIP~\cite{medclip2022} and
BioViL-T~\cite{biovilt2023} have demonstrated strong performance on chest X-ray
classification and report grounding. However, extending these approaches to 3D CT
volumes introduces fundamental challenges: processing full volumetric sequences of
tokens is computationally prohibitive, and most VLM architectures lack mechanisms
for precise 3D spatial reasoning~\cite{vilaM32024}.

VILA-M3~\cite{vilaM32024} addresses this problem by introducing an expert routing
architecture within a VLM pipeline. Rather than processing raw 3D voxels, the VLM
acts as an orchestrator that delegates spatial tasks to specialist expert models via
the MONAI VLM Agent Framework~\cite{monai2022}. This enables high-fidelity 3D
analysis without native volumetric tokenisation. Despite this architectural
advantage, VILA-M3 is pre-trained on general medical image-text data and has not
been specifically adapted for thoracic CT interpretation.

This paper proposes a targeted instruction-tuning pipeline that fine-tunes VILA-M3
on CT-RATE~\cite{ctrate2024}, a large-scale dataset of paired chest CT volumes and
radiology reports. Adaptation is achieved efficiently via LoRA~\cite{lora2022},
requiring only a fraction of the parameters of full fine-tuning. The framework's
routing mechanism directs spatial localisation queries to VISTA3D~\cite{vista3d2024},
a versatile 3D CT segmentation expert. Zero-shot transfer is then evaluated on
LIDC-IDRI~\cite{lidc2011}, an entirely unseen benchmark of expert-annotated
pulmonary nodules, alongside a case retrieval evaluation on the CT-RATE hold-out
split.

\subsection*{Key Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Routing-Specific Instruction-Tuning Pipeline.} A LoRA-based
    fine-tuning strategy that trains VILA-M3 to correctly emit structured routing
    tokens (e.g., \texttt{<VISTA3D(lung tumor)>}) in response to thoracic
    clinical prompts derived from CT-RATE report pairs. Without this adaptation,
    the pre-trained model does not reliably trigger the correct expert pathway for
    chest CT queries.

  \item \textbf{First Zero-Shot Pulmonary Nodule Detection via VLM Routing on
    LIDC-IDRI.} Pulmonary nodules are too small for native VLM spatial resolution.
    We demonstrate that routing to VISTA3D---prompted by the VLM's structured
    output---overcomes this limitation and enables the first VLM-based zero-shot
    detection evaluation on LIDC-IDRI. VISTA3D produces voxel-level segmentation
    masks from which bounding boxes are derived and evaluated using IoU and mAP.

  \item \textbf{Dual-Task Unified Pipeline.} A single fine-tuned model handles
    both segmentation routing and natural-language case retrieval. The same
    LoRA adapter is trained jointly with a causal-LM detection-routing
    objective and a CLIP-style symmetric InfoNCE objective that aligns each
    volume's image-side and text-side hidden representations, so embedding-space
    retrieval becomes meaningful without any auxiliary text generation.
\end{enumerate}


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Related Work}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Vision-Language Models in Radiology}

Contrastive image-text pretraining, established by CLIP~\cite{clip2021}, has
been adapted to the medical domain by methods such as MedCLIP~\cite{medclip2022}
and BioViL-T~\cite{biovilt2023}. These approaches learn representations from
unpaired or temporally paired radiology report-image corpora and achieve
competitive zero-shot classification performance on chest X-ray benchmarks.
More recent large multimodal models such as GPT-4V~\cite{gpt4_2023} and
Med-Gemini extend report-grounded reasoning to open-ended clinical question
answering. However, these models remain constrained to 2D input modalities and
lack mechanisms for volumetric spatial grounding.

\subsection{3D Medical Image Analysis}

Volumetric CT analysis has been dominated by convolutional architectures.
nnU-Net~\cite{nnunet2021} established a robust self-configuring baseline for 3D
segmentation across diverse clinical tasks. For pulmonary nodule detection
specifically, 3D Faster R-CNN-based systems and sliding-window CNNs have set
strong supervised benchmarks on LIDC-IDRI~\cite{lidc2011}. VISTA3D~\cite{vista3d2024}
extends this paradigm with a promptable 3D segmentation model that accepts text
and point prompts, making it well-suited as a spatially-precise expert in a
VLM routing architecture.

\subsection{Expert-Routed and Agent-Based Medical VLMs}

VILA-M3~\cite{vilaM32024} builds on the VILA visual language architecture~\cite{vila2024}
and the LLaMA language backbone~\cite{llama2023} to introduce expert routing:
the VLM's language head generates structured routing signals that invoke
specialist models for tasks requiring high-resolution spatial reasoning. The
MONAI VLM Agent Framework~\cite{monai2022} provides the infrastructure for
this orchestration. Our work extends VILA-M3 to thoracic CT by fine-tuning the
routing and language components on domain-specific data, and performs the first
systematic zero-shot segmentation evaluation of the resulting system on
LIDC-IDRI---a benchmark that no VLM has previously addressed due to the
fine-grained spatial scale of pulmonary nodules.

\section{Proposed Methodology}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Dataset Configuration}

The framework employs a dual-dataset strategy that strictly separates
adaptation from evaluation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CT-RATE}~\cite{ctrate2024}: A large-scale dataset of over
    50{,}000 reconstructed 3D chest CT volumes paired with structured radiology
    reports. CT-RATE serves as the training corpus for instruction-tuning.
    Owing to the storage constraints of the available server we operate on a
    randomly sampled subset of approximately 10{,}000 volumes; a 10\%
    held-out split (about 1{,}000 volumes) is reserved exclusively for case
    retrieval evaluation and never seen during adaptation.

  \item \textbf{LIDC-IDRI}~\cite{lidc2011}: A reference database of 1{,}018
    CT scans with expert-consensus nodule annotations from four independent
    radiologists. A subset of 115 scans with $\geq$3-radiologist consensus
    nodules is used for evaluation; this dataset is withheld entirely from
    training and used only for zero-shot detection benchmarking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Architecture and Dynamic Routing}

The framework positions VILA-M3 as an intelligent orchestrator between the
clinical query and specialist processing pipelines. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}
provides an overview of the system.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\columnwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
  \caption{Overview of the proposed framework. VILA-M3 receives multimodal
    input (key CT slices and clinical query) and classifies query intent. Localisation
    queries are routed to VISTA3D via the MONAI Agent Framework; retrieval queries
    are resolved through embedding-space matching. The LoRA fine-tuning stage
    (CT-RATE) is applied to both pathways.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The routing pipeline proceeds in three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Global Context Extraction.} VILA-M3 receives the user query
    (e.g., \textit{``Localise the pulmonary nodule in this CT scan''}) together
    with a compact volumetric representation of the CT input, constructed by
    sampling key axial slices at uniform anatomical intervals.

  \item \textbf{Task Classification and Routing.} The model classifies the
    query intent and emits a structured routing token in its output. Segmentation
    queries produce a token of the form \texttt{<VISTA3D(lung tumor)>}, which the
    MONAI VLM Agent Framework intercepts and dispatches to VISTA3D. Retrieval
    queries are handled internally through the VLM's embedding space.

  \item \textbf{Expert Execution and Response Synthesis.} VISTA3D processes the
    full-resolution CT volume with the \texttt{lung tumor} class prompt and
    returns a voxel-level segmentation mask. The framework converts this mask into
    a 2D overlay image and a textual colour description, which is passed back to
    VILA-M3 as a new context turn. Bounding boxes are derived from the mask
    extents for detection evaluation. The VLM synthesises a final,
    language-anchored clinical response from this structured expert output. For
    retrieval queries, the VLM embedding is compared against the CT-RATE corpus
    index using cosine similarity.
\end{enumerate}

This separation of concerns preserves the VLM's semantic reasoning capacity
while delegating computationally expensive volumetric processing to a
purpose-built expert, avoiding the memory infeasibility of native 3D token
processing.

\subsection{Instruction Fine-Tuning Strategy}

We apply Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\cite{lora2022} to the VILA-M3 8B
checkpoint (\texttt{MONAI/Llama3-VILA-M3-8B}), targeting the query and value
projection matrices of the language-model attention layers only (the vision
tower and multimodal projector remain frozen). The LoRA rank is set to
$r = 16$ with a scaling factor $\alpha = 32$. A single adapter is trained
\emph{jointly} with two complementary objectives so that one fine-tuned model
serves both downstream tasks:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Detection-routing objective (causal-LM loss).} Input: a prompt
    of the form \textit{``Identify and localise thoracic abnormalities in the
    provided chest CT scan.''} paired with key axial slices. Output: a response
    that emits the structured routing token \texttt{<VISTA3D(lung tumor)>} when
    the paired report mentions pulmonary findings, followed by a clinical
    summary. The loss is the standard next-token cross-entropy on the response
    tokens, masking the prompt prefix and image-placeholder positions.

  \item \textbf{Retrieval objective (symmetric contrastive InfoNCE).} For each
    volume in the batch we compute (i) an \emph{image-side} embedding from the
    key slices paired with a neutral prompt and (ii) a \emph{text-side}
    embedding from the radiology report, both taken as the last-token hidden
    state of the LLaMA body. Embeddings are $\ell_2$-normalised and compared
    via a CLIP-style symmetric InfoNCE loss with in-batch negatives, pulling
    each volume's image embedding towards its own report and away from the
    other volumes in the batch. This is what enables embedding-space retrieval;
    no auxiliary text generation is used for retrieval.
\end{itemize}

Training is performed on a single NVIDIA A100 80\,GB GPU under CUDA 11.6 /
driver 470, requiring the PyTorch cu118 build. The VILA-M3 8B model occupies
approximately 17\,GB in bf16, leaving substantial headroom for activation
storage; VISTA3D is loaded only at inference time. Gradient checkpointing is
applied to the language-model body to fit the contrastive batch alongside the
detection forward. The AdamW optimiser is used with a learning rate of
$1 \times 10^{-4}$, a cosine annealing schedule with 3\% warm-up, weight decay
0.01, and an effective detection batch size of 4 (per-device batch 1, gradient
accumulation 4); the contrastive objective uses an in-batch size of 8 with 8
key slices per volume and softmax temperature $\tau = 0.07$. Disk constraints
on the available server (241\,GB) require streaming CT-RATE volumes one at a
time and discarding the raw NIfTI after extracting key axial slices. The
present version of this paper reports results trained on a subset of 1{,}797
CT-RATE volumes with a 200-volume hold-out reserved for retrieval evaluation;
a scaled run on approximately 9{,}000 train / 1{,}000 hold-out volumes is in
progress and will replace these numbers in the final manuscript.

\section{Experimental Setup and Evaluation}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Baseline Models}

The adapted framework is compared against two categories of baselines:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Detection baselines.} Supervised 3D models trained directly on
    LIDC-IDRI annotations: nnU-Net~\cite{nnunet2021} as a self-configuring
    upper bound, and VISTA3D invoked directly with \texttt{lung tumor} without
    VLM routing, to isolate the contribution of instruction-tuned routing over
    na\"ive expert invocation.

  \item \textbf{Retrieval baselines.} CT-CLIP~\cite{ctrate2024}, a published
    CT report--image contrastive model trained on CT-RATE, as a direct
    domain-matched baseline; and the pre-fine-tuned VILA-M3 8B checkpoint
    (no CT-RATE LoRA) to measure the gain from routing-specific adaptation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\textbf{Zero-Shot Abnormality Detection (LIDC-IDRI).}
Detection is performed via voxel-level segmentation: VISTA3D produces a
binary mask for the \texttt{lung tumor} class, which is evaluated against
the four-radiologist consensus contours from LIDC-IDRI. Performance is
measured using Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) as the primary metric and
Intersection over Union (IoU) as the secondary metric. These overlap-based
metrics are the standard for segmentation-based detection in medical imaging
and directly reflect the spatial precision of nodule localisation.

\textbf{Text-to-Image Case Retrieval (CT-RATE hold-out).}
Performance is measured using Recall@$K$ for $K \in \{1, 5, 10\}$. In clinical
triage, retrieving the correct historical case within the top-$K$ results
translates directly to workflow efficiency; Recall@$K$ provides a direct measure
of the model's practical utility for semantic case search.

\subsection{Results}

We separately measure (i) the model's ability to emit the expected expert-call
\emph{structure}, (ii) its ability to bind the structurally-correct call to
the \emph{correct class}, and (iii) the resulting segmentation overlap. Decoupling
structure from class is what surfaces the most informative behaviour of the
fine-tuned VLM: as we show below, the call format is learned completely while
class identity is learned only partially.

\paragraph{Detection.}
Table~\ref{tab:detection} reports the three detection conditions. The
pre-fine-tuned VILA-M3 \emph{never} emits the expected routing token; manual
inspection of its outputs shows malformed pseudo-calls of the form
\texttt{<Lung()>}, \texttt{<LUL()>}, and similar, none of which match the
expert-call grammar required by the MONAI agent framework. After joint LoRA
fine-tuning, the model emits a structurally well-formed
\texttt{<VISTA3D(\textellipsis)>} call on \textbf{every} evaluated scan
(format precision $100.00\%$). However, only $15.65\%$ of those calls bind to
the correct class \texttt{lung tumor}; the rest emit semantically related but
mismatched targets such as \texttt{<VISTA3D(skeleton)>},
\texttt{<VISTA3D(sarcoidosis)>}, and \texttt{<VISTA3D(lung tumor, pancreas
tumor)>}. The fine-tuned model has therefore acquired the routing
\emph{format} completely but has acquired the correct routing \emph{class}
only partially.

Direct VISTA3D invocation with the \texttt{lung tumor} prompt reaches Dice
$0.273$ on the same scans. The routed VLM achieves a mean Dice of $0.040$
because un-invoked scans contribute zero; verifying via
$\text{class precision} \times \text{direct Dice} = 0.1565 \times 0.273
\approx 0.043$ closely matches the measured $0.040$. This decomposition
licenses a clean analytical statement: \emph{when correct routing occurs,
the fine-tuned VLM achieves the per-scan upper bound set by VISTA3D
itself}. The remaining headroom on this benchmark is therefore entirely a
function of class-precision, not of segmentation quality.

\paragraph{Retrieval.}
Table~\ref{tab:retrieval} shows that the joint contrastive objective
\emph{decreases the training loss substantially} (from
$\ln 8 \approx 2.08$ at initialisation to $\approx 1.29$ at the end of three
epochs) but \emph{does not transfer} to held-out retrieval. Both the
pre-fine-tuned baseline and the LoRA-adapted model sit within noise of the
$1/200$ random-chance reference. We read this as a measurement of the
data-scale limit of single-adapter contrastive transfer on a generative VLM
of this size: with $1{,}797$ training pairs, the contrastive objective fits
the training distribution but does not generalise to unseen image--text
pairs. Comparative dual-encoder baselines such as CT-CLIP are trained on
$50{,}000$+ paired volumes; our subset is below the threshold at which
contrastive adaptation of an 8\,B-parameter generative model produces a
transferable retrieval embedding under in-batch negatives alone.

\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{Routing format versus class identity.}
Decomposing the routing rate into a structural (format) component and a
semantic (class) component reveals where instruction tuning succeeds and where
it falls short. The format -- a closed grammar of less than one token's
combinatorial complexity once the assistant-turn header is in place -- is
learned essentially in full. The class identity, in contrast, requires the
model to bind the cue from the visual stream and the prompt to one specific
expert class label, and at the present training scale that binding is
unreliable. This finding is consistent with prior observations that
small-corpus instruction tuning teaches a response \emph{shape} faster than
it teaches the response \emph{content}, and it directly motivates two natural
follow-ups: (i)~scaling the routed-instruction training corpus, which the
in-progress 9{,}000-volume run will directly test, and (ii)~constraining the
sampling at inference time to the closed set of VISTA3D class names, which
would convert format precision directly into class precision.

\paragraph{Routing condition is bounded by the expert.}
Because a correctly routed scan invokes exactly the same VISTA3D bundle as the
direct-invocation baseline, the routed VLM cannot exceed the direct VISTA3D
Dice of $0.273$ on \emph{any} scan it correctly routes. The routed mean Dice
is therefore exactly $\text{class precision} \times 0.273$ up to small
sub-population variance; we observe $0.040$ against the predicted $0.043$. The
practical implication is that the contribution of the VLM in this framework
is the \emph{delegation decision}, not the segmentation itself: a routing-rate
improvement is the only lever available to improve detection numbers under
the current architecture. Segmentation quality is a property of VISTA3D.

\paragraph{Data scale and contrastive transfer.}
The retrieval result is most usefully read as a characterisation rather than
as a failed target. A generative VLM was never trained for cross-modal
retrieval; the present work asks whether a single LoRA adapter trained
jointly with a contrastive objective at modest scale is sufficient to make
its hidden space retrieval-aligned. The answer is no at $1{,}797$ pairs, and
training-loss curves indicate the model can fit the training distribution
without that fit transferring. This is informative for the deployment-time
question of when a generative VLM can be re-used as an embedding model for
retrieval and when a dedicated dual-encoder is still required.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
The reported numbers are derived from $1{,}797$ training pairs and a 200-volume
retrieval hold-out; the scaled run on approximately $9{,}000$ training pairs
with a $1{,}000$-volume hold-out, currently in progress, will replace these
in the final manuscript. The 115-scan LIDC evaluation set is constrained by
the storage budget of the available shared server and by the requirement that
each evaluated scan have a $\geq$3-radiologist consensus nodule; a larger
evaluation would tighten the reported Dice but would not change the routing
behaviour of the model. A supervised nnU-Net upper bound on the same LIDC
subset is left as an immediate extension. Beyond scaling, we identify
class-constrained decoding of the expert-call token as the most direct route
to lifting class precision toward the per-scan ceiling.


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presents a parameter-efficient adaptation framework for expert-routed
vision-language models applied to 3D chest CT analysis. By fine-tuning VILA-M3 8B
on CT-RATE using LoRA and leveraging the MONAI VLM Agent Framework's built-in
VISTA3D routing, the framework addresses the fundamental challenge of applying
VLMs to volumetric medical data without native 3D processing. Evaluation on the
zero-shot LIDC-IDRI benchmark and the CT-RATE retrieval hold-out assesses
whether thoracic instruction tuning yields clinically meaningful improvements
over the pre-fine-tuned baseline. This approach has the potential to assist
radiologists in initial triage and semantic case retrieval, reducing dependence
on costly dense annotations. Future work will investigate multi-expert routing
strategies and extension to additional thoracic pathologies beyond pulmonary
nodules.
